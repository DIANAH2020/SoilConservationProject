SOIL CONSERVATION METHODS
Contour Farming

Contour farming in action

Plowing along the contour lines of slopes instead of vertically reduces water runoff and soil erosion. This method slows down the flow of water across the land, allowing it to infiltrate into the soil.
Terracing

How can terracing be applied to control soil erosion

Building terraces on slopes helps to create flat areas, reducing the speed of water runoff and preventing soil erosion. Terraces also help in retaining water and nutrients within the soil.

This method of farming and soil conservation practiced on hills and sloped lands. It was traditionally used by the Incas and is widely practiced around the world today. It involves the building of platforms, forming step-like structures along a slope.

Buffer Strips

Using Buffer strips for soil conservation

Buffer strips are narrow plantings of perennial plants that are primarily used to reduce water runoff from fields, including loss of pesticides and fertilizers. They can also help provide habitat for pollinators and other beneficial insects as well as wildlife.
Windbreaks

Using windbreaks to conserve soil

A windbreak or shelterbelt is a vegetation barrier designed to reduce or eliminate the velocity of the wind and hence reduce wind erosion. (Windbreaks are also used for snow control and to provide shelter for buildings and livestock).

Windbreaks are generally planted on the west, southwest, or northwest boundary of a field to provide protection from prevailing winds. The protection is maximized downwind of the barrier, where the wind speed is significantly reduced for a distance of 15 to 20 times the height of the trees. The windward side of the break receives protection for 3 to 5 times the total height.

The project aims to promote sustainable soil conservation practices to enhance agricultural productivity while preserving natural resources.
